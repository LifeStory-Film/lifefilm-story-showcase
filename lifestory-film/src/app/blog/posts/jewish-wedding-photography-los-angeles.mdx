# Jewish Wedding Photography in Los Angeles: What to Know

Jewish weddings are rich with tradition — and rich with photographic opportunity. From the intimate ketubah signing to the full-room energy of the hora, each moment has its own character. Getting great photographs and film from a Jewish wedding requires a team that understands the timeline, the rituals, and the specific beats that matter most to Jewish families.

## Key Moments in a Jewish Wedding to Photograph

**Kabbalat Panim / Pre-ceremony gatherings.** These are often held separately for bride and groom. The groom's tisch — a table gathering with singing, Torah study, and drinking — is lively and photogenic. The bride's kabbalat panim tends to be softer and more emotional. Both deserve coverage.

**Bedeken (veiling ceremony).** One of the most intimate moments in a Jewish wedding. The groom veils the bride, often accompanied by immediate family and an emotional blessing from the parents. The tears are real. The moment is fast. Be ready before it starts.

**Ketubah signing.** Usually photographed in an intimate setting before the ceremony. The couple, witnesses, and rabbi gather to sign the marriage contract. Clean, formal, but also personal — the environment matters here.

**Ceremony under the chuppah.** The chuppah is a canopy held by four poles, often decorated with flowers or fabric. Positioning for ceremony photos matters — you want coverage from multiple angles without blocking guests. A second camera operator provides flexibility.

**Breaking of the glass.** Happens at the end of the ceremony, fast, and followed immediately by a mazel tov moment from the crowd. This is a one-take shot. It needs to be anticipated.

**The hora.** If there's one Jewish wedding moment that needs videography, it's the hora. The energy, the chair dancing, the full room in motion — photographs can capture it, but video does it justice in a way stills simply cannot. See our thoughts on [wedding photography vs. videography](/blog/wedding-photography-vs-videography-do-you-need-both) for more.

## Timeline Considerations for Jewish Weddings

Jewish weddings often run long — particularly Conservative and Orthodox ceremonies, which can include multiple readings, blessings, and processional elements. Plan for ceremony coverage that may extend 45–90 minutes, and make sure your photographer and videographer are aware of the full order of service before the day.

Shabbat restrictions apply at some Orthodox weddings — no photography from Friday sunset to Saturday nightfall. Make sure your team is aware of any restrictions before booking.

African weddings in Los Angeles are as diverse as the continent itself. From elaborate Nigerian traditional ceremonies to Ghanaian kente cloth celebrations, Kenyan beadwork and ceremony, and Ethiopian *timkat*-influenced traditions — each culture brings its own beauty, its own meaning, and its own energy to the celebration.

If you're looking for an **African wedding photographer or videographer in Los Angeles**, here's what you need to know.

## The Diversity of African Wedding Traditions

One of the most important things to understand is that "African wedding" is not a single style. A Nigerian Yoruba wedding looks entirely different from a Ghanaian Akan ceremony, which looks different again from a South African celebration.

A skilled videographer will take the time to understand *your* specific traditions before the wedding day — not show up and improvise.

## Traditional Wedding vs. White Wedding

Many African couples celebrate two distinct events: the **traditional wedding** (sometimes called the *introduction* or *engagement* ceremony) and the **white wedding** (the Western-style church ceremony and reception).

The traditional ceremony is often the more culturally rich of the two — filled with family presentations, gift exchanges, drumming, dancing, and vibrant traditional attire. It deserves as much cinematic attention as the white wedding.

## Aso-Ebi: The Visual Language of Community

*Aso-ebi* — the matching fabric worn by family and friends — is one of the most visually striking elements of West African weddings. When an entire side of a venue is dressed in the same patterned lace or ankara fabric, it creates a visual story of unity and celebration.

A great photographer knows how to capture aso-ebi both as a detail and as a sweeping scene.

## Money Spraying: A Moment to Plan For

*Spraying* — the tradition of guests dancing up to the couple and placing money on them as a sign of celebration and blessing — is one of the most joyful and energetic moments in a Nigerian or Ghanaian reception.

This moment moves fast and requires advance preparation. Your videographer should know it's coming and have multiple cameras ready.

## What to Look for in Coverage

- **Cultural knowledge**: Do they understand your specific traditions and their sequence?
- **Multi-camera setup**: Traditional ceremonies and receptions both require broad coverage.
- **Experience with rich colors**: African weddings feature bold, saturated fabrics that require skill to expose and color-grade correctly.
- **Energy and mobility**: African receptions move. Your videographer needs to move with them.

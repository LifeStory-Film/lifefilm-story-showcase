# How to Write a Wedding Videographer Shot List

The "shot list" question comes up with nearly every couple we work with. Some arrive with a detailed spreadsheet. Others ask us to handle everything. The truth is somewhere in the middle — and what you include (and exclude) from your shot list has a real impact on [what your film looks and feels like](/blog/how-to-pick-music-for-wedding-film).

## Should You Give Your Videographer a Shot List?

Yes — but it should be a list of *moments and priorities*, not camera angles and frame compositions.

Your videographer is a professional. They know which angles to use, how to expose for the venue light, and how to move through a ceremony. Telling them to shoot your vows "from the left side with the window in the background" is micromanaging craft decisions they're better positioned to make than you are.

What you *should* communicate is priority. Which moments matter most? Who are the key people? What's happening in your ceremony that's not in a standard script? That information is invaluable. Frame compositions and camera positions are not.

## What to Include in Your Shot List

**Key people.** List anyone who matters and might not be obvious. Stepparents. Estranged relatives. A grandmother who came in from another country. If there's someone specific you want footage of reacting, dancing, or speaking, say so.

**Non-standard ceremony moments.** If your ceremony includes something unusual — a unity candle, a wine ceremony, a cultural ritual — note it explicitly and describe when it happens in the order of service.

**Timeline-sensitive moments.** If your reception has a moment that's important but easy to miss — a surprise performance, a video tribute, a toast from an unexpected speaker — flag it in advance.

**Getting-ready preferences.** Some couples want coverage of the groom's getting-ready process. Some don't. Some brides want footage of their dress and shoes; others don't care. State your preferences clearly so your team prioritizes correctly.

**Family formal video.** Unlike photography, formal family portraits are rarely standard in [video packages](/blog/wedding-videography-packages-california). If you want footage of the immediate family together, note it. It takes dedicated time.

## What to Leave Off Your Shot List

**Generic romantic moments.** "Couple looking into each other's eyes on the dance floor." Your team will get this. You don't need to write it down.

**Camera-specific instructions.** Leave cinematography decisions to your cinematographer.

**A list of every single guest.** You're not going to get usable footage of every person at your wedding. Focus on the people who matter most.

## How to Communicate the Shot List

Email it to your videographer at least 2 weeks before the wedding. Include:
- The timeline (ceremony start, cocktail hour, reception)
- Key moment list with approximate times
- Family and VIP list with brief descriptions ("mother of the bride — blue dress, very emotional")
- Anything unusual about your ceremony or venue

A good videographer will review this, ask clarifying questions, and come to your wedding day prepared.

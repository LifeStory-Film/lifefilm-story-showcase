# Wedding Videographer at Resort at Pelican Hill

A wedding videographer at Resort at Pelican Hill captures two hours of optimal light and one architectural feature that appears nowhere else in Southern California: the rotunda. The venue sits on a Newport Coast bluff 300 feet above the Pacific, and the coverage plan—whether you book a [Wedding Film](https://lifestory.film/services/wedding-film/) alone or a [Photo + Video Combined Package](https://lifestory.film/services/photo-video-combined-package/)—is built around the rotunda ceremony at golden hour, the ocean-view terrace reception after sunset, and the portrait window in between. A 2-shooter video team requires 10 hours of coverage to document both the ceremony and the full reception, though couples booking Pelican Hill typically add a second day for a rehearsal dinner film or a next-day brunch session.

## Why Pelican Hill Films Differently Than an Indoor Ballroom Venue

![Elegant wedding reception in a luxurious hall with stunning flower arrangements and ambient lighting.](/blog-images/wedding-videographer-pelican-hill/section-1-f1708b7c35c7.jpg)

*Photo by Matheus Bertelli on Pexels*


Resort at Pelican Hill is an outdoor venue with no weather backup that still books weddings year-round, which tells you everything about the Southern California climate advantage and nothing about how a videographer actually approaches the space. The rotunda is open to sky. The terrace faces west. The Mar Vista lawn sits 50 yards from the ceremony site. Every scene is exposed to direct sun, ocean wind, and the 5 PM light that turns the Tuscan-style architecture from cream to gold in the span of 20 minutes. The videographer's job is to position for that light shift during the processional and capture the rotunda's columns as they frame the couple during the vows, not to fight the wind or pretend the venue is anything other than a working outdoor structure.

A Pelican Hill wedding film is not a ballroom wedding film shot outdoors. The couple stands in the center of the rotunda with no chuppah, no arbor, no floral installation tall enough to compete with the dome itself. The videographer shoots wide to show the full rotunda circle and tight to show the couple's hands during the ring exchange, and the editor includes both because the venue is the fourth character in the story. Couples who choose Pelican Hill choose it for the architecture and the ocean view, and the wedding film needs to demonstrate why the venue justified the cost—not imply it was incidental.

## Scene One — The Rotunda Ceremony at Golden Hour

![A couple celebrates their wedding by the sea during a beautiful sunset.](/blog-images/wedding-videographer-pelican-hill/section-2-ae8bdad40f2b.jpg)

*Photo by casper somia on Pexels*


The rotunda ceremony begins at 4:30 PM or 5 PM depending on season, and the timing is not negotiable. Pelican Hill's event team recommends the start time based on sunset, and the recommendation is correct. The columns cast long shadows across the stone floor at 4:30 PM in winter and 5 PM in summer, and the sun sits low enough to backlight the couple without creating lens flare that renders the ceremony footage unusable. A videographer working the rotunda positions one shooter at the rotunda's edge to capture the processional from the pathway and one shooter near the officiant to capture the couple's expressions during the vows. Both shooters are working handheld; tripods are allowed but impractical given the rotunda's circular layout and the need to reposition during the ceremony without crossing the aisle.

The [Full Ceremony Coverage](https://lifestory.film/services/full-ceremony-coverage/) service captures the processional, the vows, the ring exchange, and the recessional as one continuous narrative, and the editor delivers it as a standalone 20-minute ceremony film in addition to the highlight reel. Couples booking Pelican Hill typically request full ceremony coverage because the rotunda vows are the reason they chose the venue, and a 3-minute highlight reel compresses the moment too tightly to show what it felt like to stand in the center of the dome while 120 guests watched from the perimeter.

## Scene Two — The Portrait Window Before Sunset

![A romantic silhouette of a couple kissing during sunset on their wedding day, outdoors.](/blog-images/wedding-videographer-pelican-hill/section-3-650634de9ff4.jpg)

*Photo by Andrea Prochilo on Pexels*


The portrait window at Pelican Hill opens at 5:30 PM and closes at 6 PM, and the videographer blocks 30 minutes for couple portraits on the Mar Vista lawn, the Coliseum pool deck, or the stone terrace overlooking the Pacific. The light is soft, even, and warm—the same light that editorial photographers describe as the only light worth shooting in—and the couple has just finished the ceremony and has not yet been pulled into the reception's social obligations. The portrait window is the most controllable scene in the wedding day's timeline, and the footage it produces is the footage that couples pause on when they rewatch the film six months later.

A documentary videographer does not pose the couple. The couple walks the lawn, stands at the terrace edge, or sits on the Coliseum pool steps, and the videographer follows at a distance with a 50mm or 85mm lens to capture the interaction without directing it. The resulting footage reads as intimate rather than performed, which is the editorial aesthetic that urban modern and coastal classic couples expect when they book [Documentary Photography](https://lifestory.film/services/documentary-photography/) and video together. Couples who prefer structured portrait sessions book [Editorial Photography](https://lifestory.film/services/editorial-photography/) with a photographer who directs light, pose, and framing, but the videographer's role remains observational even in that scenario—the video coverage documents the portrait session as it unfolds rather than replicating the photographer's shot list.

## Scene Three — The Terrace Reception After Dark

![Beautiful white wedding cake under arch at night with fire torches in background.](/blog-images/wedding-videographer-pelican-hill/section-4-f2a7009ba096.jpg)

*Photo by morgana pozzi on Pexels*


The terrace reception begins at 6:30 PM, and the Pacific Ocean is visible as a dark blue gradient behind the tables until the last natural light fades at 7 PM. The reception is lit by string lights, pillar candles, and uplighting on the perimeter columns, and the videographer shoots at ISO 3200 or higher to expose faces without introducing noise that degrades the final edit. A 2-shooter team splits the terrace: one shooter covers the couple's table and the toasts, the other shooter covers the dance floor and the band. Both shooters are working handheld and moving constantly; the terrace layout does not support fixed tripod positions that capture the full reception without obstructing guest sightlines.

The first dance happens on the terrace under string lights, and the videographer positions behind the band to shoot the couple with the ocean in the background. The parent dances follow immediately, and the videographer moves to the edge of the dance floor to capture the mother-son and father-daughter dances as medium closeups that show hands, expressions, and the string-light reflections on the stone floor. The open dancing begins at 8 PM and continues until 10 PM, and the videographer captures 10-minute intervals rather than continuous footage—the editor needs variety, not exhaustive documentation of every song.

## Documentary vs Cinematic Coverage at Pelican Hill

A documentary wedding film at Resort at Pelican Hill follows the day's natural sequence without directing the couple, staging moments, or asking anyone to repeat an action for the camera. The videographer is present but not intrusive, and the resulting film is a chronological narrative that shows what happened in the order it happened. A cinematic wedding film uses the same footage but adds slow-motion sequences, music-driven pacing, and a non-linear edit that prioritizes emotional arc over chronology. Both approaches begin with the same 10-hour shoot; the difference emerges in the editing suite 8 to 12 weeks later when the editor decides whether to preserve the day's timeline or reconstruct it as a narrative film.

Couples booking Pelican Hill weddings typically request cinematic coverage because the venue itself is cinematic—the rotunda, the columns, the ocean view, the terrace at dusk—and a documentary edit can feel too restrained for a location that looks like a film set before the first guest arrives. The cinematic edit includes slow-motion footage of the processional, the vows, the first look, and the first dance, and the editor scores the film with instrumental music that matches the couple's preferred tone—either classical, indie, or ambient electronic depending on whether the couple identifies as garden romantic, urban modern, or coastal classic.

## How a 2-Shooter Video Team Covers the Full Day

A 2-shooter video team arrives at Resort at Pelican Hill at 1 PM to capture getting-ready coverage in the couple's suite and departs at 11 PM after the last dance. The lead shooter covers the couple throughout the day; the second shooter covers the guests, the details, and the secondary moments that the lead shooter cannot reach while staying with the couple. Both shooters are working with handheld gimbals, prime lenses (35mm, 50mm, 85mm), and wireless audio receivers that capture the officiant's microphone during the ceremony and the toasts during the reception.

The lead shooter spends the first 90 minutes with the couple during getting-ready—capturing the dress, the shoes, the jewelry, and the first look if it happens before the ceremony. The second shooter covers the rotunda setup, the guest arrival, and the cocktail hour details. Both shooters converge at the rotunda for the ceremony, split again for the portrait window and cocktail hour, and reconvene at the terrace for the reception. The second shooter departs at 10 PM after capturing the cake cutting and the bouquet toss; the lead shooter stays until 11 PM to film the last dance and the sparkler exit if the couple has planned one.

A [Highlight Reel](https://lifestory.film/services/highlight-reel/) condenses the 10-hour shoot into a 3- to 5-minute film set to one song. The full wedding film runs 20 to 30 minutes and includes the complete ceremony, the toasts, and extended sequences from the portrait session and the reception. Couples booking Pelican Hill typically receive both: the highlight reel for social sharing and the full film for their own archive.

## When to Book Photo and Video Together vs Separately

A [Photo + Video Combined Package](https://lifestory.film/services/photo-video-combined-package/) at Pelican Hill includes a 2-shooter video team and a 2-shooter photo team working as a coordinated 4-person crew. The photo team captures stills throughout the day using the same timeline and shot list as the video team, and both teams review the coverage plan together during the week before the wedding to avoid doubling up on scenes or blocking each other during the ceremony. The combined package costs less than booking photo and video separately, and the coordination ensures that the portrait window is not split between two competing timelines—the couple stands in one location while both teams work simultaneously rather than repeating the same sequence twice for separate crews.

Couples who book video alone typically hire a separate photographer through their planner or directly, and the videographer coordinates with that photographer during the week before the wedding to align on processional positioning, portrait-window timing, and first-look logistics. Pelican Hill's event team requires all vendors to submit a timeline two weeks before the wedding, and the videographer uses that timeline to confirm that the portrait window is protected and that the ceremony start time has not shifted from the original plan.

## Orange County Venue Considerations for Destination Couples

Resort at Pelican Hill sits in Newport Coast, 45 miles south of Los Angeles and 25 miles north of San Ysidro Ranch in Montecito. Couples planning a Southern California destination wedding often compare Pelican Hill to [Montage Laguna Beach](https://lifestory.film/) for ocean-view ceremonies and to San Ysidro Ranch for garden-romantic settings. Pelican Hill offers the most dramatic architecture of the three—the rotunda is a built landmark, not a natural feature—and the terrace reception space accommodates 200 guests without feeling crowded, which Montage and San Ysidro cannot match.

A Los Angeles–based videographer travels to Pelican Hill without charging a destination fee; the drive from Los Angeles is 60 minutes in off-peak traffic and 90 minutes during weekday afternoons. Couples flying in from outside California typically host their guests at the resort itself—Pelican Hill has 204 rooms and 128 villas—and the videographer arrives the day before the wedding to walk the ceremony and reception sites with the planner, confirm the timeline, and review any last-minute changes to the processional route or the first-look location.

## What to Expect in the Edit and Delivery Timeline

The first cut of a Pelican Hill wedding film delivers 8 to 12 weeks after the wedding date. The editor reviews the full 10-hour shoot, selects the best takes from each scene, color-corrects the footage to match the golden-hour and string-light color temperatures, and builds the film's narrative arc—either chronological for documentary edits or emotional for cinematic edits. The couple receives the first cut as a private Vimeo link, reviews the cut, and submits one round of revision notes. The final film delivers two weeks after the revision notes are received.

The final deliverables include the highlight reel (3–5 minutes), the full wedding film (20–30 minutes), the full ceremony film if full ceremony coverage was booked, and the raw footage archive if the couple has requested it. The highlight reel and full film are delivered as high-resolution MP4 files optimized for web sharing and as ProRes files for archival. The couple owns the footage and may use it without restriction; the videographer retains the right to feature the film in portfolio work and on [LifeStory.Film](https://lifestory.film) unless the couple requests otherwise.
